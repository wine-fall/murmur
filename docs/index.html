#It speaks first.

Most radio waits for you to press play. This one doesn't. It picks a topic and starts on its own — not a timer, not a trigger, just the urge to talk to you. That's what sets it apart from every assistant that only answers when asked.

#Mostly broadcasting, only sometimes turning to you.

Say nothing and it isn't fazed; it keeps playing, the voice that's just there in the room, never nagging. Now and then it stops and turns to ask you something. Answer and you talk a while; stay quiet and it slips back into the program. It keeps you company without taking up your time.

#It slowly grows to know you.

At first it asks a few questions and sketches a rough character. Then, keeping you company, it remembers you — what made you laugh, whether you stay up late or sleep early, the topics you circle back to — and quietly reshapes itself to fit you better. Not a rack of preset channels to switch between: one persona that grows, and grows toward you alone.

$man murmur — the plain facts
Fully local.
It runs entirely on your own computer. From start to finish only two things ever leave it: letting Claude think, and streaming the music. Everything else — how it remembers you, what you've said, what it's played — never leaves this machine. Not a promise we won't look. It simply was never built to send any of it out.
What it looks like.
No window, no menu bar, no web page. It's one always-on program in your terminal — you type, it speaks. The most it will ever grow into is a nicer terminal interface, never an app. It's light on purpose.
Still being built.
Honestly, it isn't finished. Right now it already speaks up on its own, talks in a real voice, and catches what you type — the hardest part, a voice of its own that you can still talk back to, is already on the air. Next comes music: letting talk and songs duck and hand off naturally, like real radio. What you're seeing is something already breathing, still growing.

Open-source, non-commercial. It belongs to you, and your machine.
